Iterative Design

Designing an interactive interface for Ten Lives, a vegan cat food startup

About the company

Ten Lives is a cat food company that makes cat food with animal protein but without the animals. There mission is to make sustainable and cruelty-free protein sources that are a cost-viable alternative for pet cats.

Our Design

We decided to design the mobile web interface for Ten Lives to promote and sell their products.

*Note that this is the mobile version of a webpage and not a mobile app.*

Sketches

We each sketched some designs of what we wanted the interface to look like and what we thought would create the best experience for our users.

Design 1

This design is meant to better engage with the users visually and uses design elements as well as images comvey the goal of the site. The home, about, and cart pages all include images that are relavent to make it obvious that this is a shopping site for impossible cat food.

home

The home page uses visual elements to demonstrate hierarchy and places our most important navigation sections in the middle.

about

The about page includes images of cats and plants to visually express the premise of the site.


cart

The cart has a cute image of a cat riding in a shopping cart (aww) and shows the page before any items are added.

Design 2

This design is meant to be very minimalist. Buttons are more understated and there are fewer icons/imagery and less distinctive separation between parts of the pages.

cart

The cart page displays a small image of the item along with the prices and total. The checkout button at the bottom is underlined to indicicate it is clickable.

shop

The shop page is a simple layout of each item's image, name, and price as well as an option to select a quantity and add to cart. The view cart button at the bottom is underlined.

FAQ

The FAQ page has a list of questions that can be expanded to see the answers.

Design 3

Since this is a shopping site, this design is more focused on showcasing the best products of the site, while also being more horizontally oriented and attempts to eliminate vertical space that ends up being infinitely large in a mobile site.

home

This home page makes it easier to see the top selling products and makes it more convenient to access the shopping page.

shop

This shopping page includes a nav bar at the top to help users get to what they want more easily. The items in the shop are also now implemented with horizontal scroll to get rid of infinite vertical space.

cart

The shopping cart also attempts to get rid of vertical space as much as possible and includes an easy PayPal alternative.

Design 4

This design is meant to be more "blocky" and vertically oriented with lots of buttons and a more scrollable layout.

home

The home page's different sections are separated into different blocks with a fixed navigation bar and a footer for contact information.

about

The about page features an image of the founders and their cats along with blocks separating the different sections.


checkout

The checkout has prominent form entries for each piece of information needed.

Wireframe

Next, we took the best parts across our 4 designs and combined them into a low-fi wireframe that we would use as the basis for our interactive interface.

Critique

Next, we created an interactive interface for our page and presented it to some of our peers. We collected the following initial feedback and applied the suggestions to our design.

Feedback:

- Users felt that the text on our about us page was too small, so we increased our font sizes.

- Users found that our header was inconsistent across pages and elements seemed to "jump around", so we standardized the header elements.

- Users felt that our navbar menu didn't have enough contrast, so we darkened the background to create contrast between the text and background.

- Users felt that having product categories to the left of product elements took up to much screen space on mobile, so we restructured the shop page to better fit a mobile interface.

- Because our product is specialized and niche, users wanted more information about the products they were purchasing, so we added a more in depth product details page for each product.

- Users found the checkout page with all purchase information displayed at once to be overwhelming, so we broke up the details by section.

- Users felt that our checkout process wasn't inclusive of more modern day payment methods, so we added options for Apple Pay, PayPal, and giftcards.

User Testing

We then submitted our revised interface to UserTesting.com to get additional feedback from professional user testers. The following tasks and questions were given to three users, who conducted a full walkthrough of our prototype.

  1. Without leaving the homepage, what are your initial impressions of the website? Explain your answer.
  2. Think of something else you might shop for on this website and describe it out loud. Now use this
    website to learn about what you described . Move on to the next task when you're done.
  3. Use this website to find what you described earlier. Move on to the next task when you're done.
  4. Purchase what you just found. Move on to the next task when you're done.
  5. Do you feel confident conducting business on this website? Explain your answer.

Results

Based on the results from our user testing, we made the following conclusions.

Our mobile website prototype is a platform for selling Ten Lives cat food—ultimately, we task users with purchasing items by browsing through products, finding relevant product information (like founder missions and faq), adding items to cart, and successfully checking out. Our user testers on UserTesting.com were given the following 5 tasks to complete:


Our user testers found the color palette and image to text balance very satisfactory. One user described the homepage as having an 'earthy' and 'natural' color scheme, with easy to digest 'bite-size bits of content'. One user thought more information could be helpful, as she was not familiar with sustainable products.
Our incentive for this task was to see if users could easily navigate to information pages, like About Us or FAQ to learn more about any questions they may have about the company or its products. All users were able to do so and rated the task very easy.
Some users were confused that the figma prototype wasn’t fully functional and that most buttons and links were not responsive. However, all users quickly and instinctively headed to the shop page and clicked on the add to cart button, either directly from the shop page or from the product page, which is what we were looking for.
One user struggled especially with this because of dead end links that were not responsive, and did not reach the order confirmation page. The other two users were able to navigate to the cart page and successfully place an order.
Both users that were able to reach the order confirmation page responded very or fairly confident, and one user responded not confident at all.


Overall, users had positive reviews, especially regarding the layout and color aesthetics and the navigation accessibility. One user described the prototype as buggy which hindered her from successfully completing all our tasks—one thing we could do is structure our tasks around the specific interactive components in the Figma, since users were confused that some links and buttons were not responsive. In terms of the interface, suggestions for improvement included more filters on the shopping page, as well as more product-related information in the homepage for those unfamiliar with sustainable or vegan products. This feedback seems reasonable and is in line with what our team expected.
